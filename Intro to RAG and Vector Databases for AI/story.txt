Barnaby was born on a damp Tuesday in April 2018, beneath the shade of a particularly large hosta leaf in a quiet garden in Kent. Like most of his kind, his early life was measured in inches and the quality of local lettuce. However, Barnaby possessed a wanderlust that was entirely incompatible with a top speed of 0.03 miles per hour.

In the summer of 2020, Barnaby met Matilda near a patch of wild strawberries. They were married that June in a silent, shimmering ceremony marked by the crossing of silver trails. Matilda was a homebody, content with the lush moss of the English countryside, but she saw the glint of the horizon in Barnaby’s stalks. She gave him her blessing when, in 2021, he decided to see what lay beyond the garden gate.

His journey began with a daring stowaway mission. Barnaby hitched a ride on the mud-caked tire of a Land Rover, which took him to the coast. Over the next five years, Barnaby became the world’s most improbable globetrotter. He crossed the English Channel on a freighter, hidden inside a crate of artisanal cheeses. He navigated the cobblestones of Paris, spent a winter in the vineyards of Tuscany, and eventually found his way onto a flight to Tokyo tucked into the fold of a diplomat’s trench coat.

He saw the world in high definition and slow motion. While humans rushed past the Taj Mahal, Barnaby spent three weeks crossing its marble plinth, noting every microscopic vein in the stone. He felt the spray of Victoria Falls and the dry heat of the Australian Outback, always surviving on the moisture of the morning dew and the kindness of strangers who mistook him for a lucky charm.

Barnaby’s grand odyssey came to an end in the fall of 2025. He had made his way to the coast of Portugal, drawn by the scent of the Atlantic salt. On October 14, 2025, Barnaby passed away peacefully in a sun-drenched crevice of a cliff overlooking the ocean in Sagres. He died as he had lived: slowly, deliberately, and with a view that stretched forever. He left behind a legacy of silver trails across six continents, proving that no distance is too great if you simply refuse to stop moving.
