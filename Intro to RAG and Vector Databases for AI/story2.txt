The snail was named Alder, though he did not know that at first.
He began as a soft, glistening curl beneath a flat stone in early spring, when the world was mostly damp and quiet. The earth smelled like moss and old rain, and everything important seemed to happen within the distance of a single leaf. Alder’s earliest memory was of pushing upward through cool soil, discovering that there was a sky—wide, bright, and far too large to comprehend.

For a long time, he believed the world was small.

There was the stone, the patch of clover, a rotting log that housed slow-moving beetles who spoke rarely but seemed to know everything. Alder grew, his shell spiraling outward in careful increments, each turn a record of time passing. He learned the rhythm of moisture and dryness, when to move and when to wait, how to glide silently across surfaces that seemed impossible at first.

But Alder was curious in a way that made stillness feel like a kind of suffocation.

One morning, after a long rain, he decided to leave the stone.

The journey began with a single stretch of muscle and a quiet resolve. The ground felt different just a few inches away—less familiar, less certain. Alder passed the clover and the log, venturing into grass that towered above him like a forest. Droplets clung to blades, forming shimmering globes that distorted the world into strange, curved reflections.

It was there he found his first treasure: a fragment of blue glass.

It was smooth and cold, catching light in a way nothing natural seemed to. Alder circled it for hours, tracing its edges, watching how it changed with the sun. He could not carry it, of course, but he remembered it. That was enough.

As days turned into weeks, Alder’s world expanded.

He crossed a narrow dirt path that vibrated with distant thunder—though later he would learn it was the sound of passing footsteps. He climbed the rough bark of a fallen branch and discovered the sensation of height, looking out over a field that seemed endless. He met ants who marched with purpose, a caterpillar who spoke of transformation, and a toad who warned him that not everything in the world was kind.

Alder listened, but he did not turn back.

By mid-summer, he reached a garden.

It was unlike anything he had seen. The ground was soft and rich, the air thick with the scent of flowers and vegetables. Leaves spread wide like open hands, and everywhere there was abundance. Alder moved carefully, tasting the edges of lettuce, the damp underside of squash leaves, the delicate petals that fell like confetti.

It was in this garden, on June 14th of his second year, that Alder met Liora.

She was smaller than him, her shell marked with pale, almost golden lines that seemed to glow in the evening light. She moved with a calm confidence, as though she understood the garden in a way Alder never could.

“You’re far from where you started,” she said, after watching him struggle over a ridge of soil.

“I think I’m just getting started,” Alder replied.

They spent days together, then weeks. Liora showed him hidden paths beneath leaves, where moisture lingered even under harsh sun. She introduced him to the best feeding spots and taught him how to read the subtle signs of weather in the air.

Alder, in turn, told her stories.

He spoke of the blue glass, the towering grass forest, the distant vibrations of the path. Liora listened with a quiet intensity, and sometimes, when the night was particularly still, she would ask him to describe those places again.

On August 3rd of that same year, beneath a broad cabbage leaf in the heart of the garden, Alder and Liora were married.

There was no ceremony as larger creatures might understand it, but there was intention and shared presence. They circled one another slowly, touching gently, exchanging something deeper than words. The garden hummed softly around them, and for Alder, the vastness of the world seemed, for a moment, perfectly contained.

They made a home beneath the cabbage leaf.

It was a good place—cool, protected, close to food and water. Time passed in a comfortable rhythm. Alder still felt the pull of exploration, but it softened, balanced by the warmth of companionship.

Their first child hatched on April 21st of the following year.

They named him Brin. He was small and translucent, his shell barely formed, his movements uncertain. Alder watched him with a kind of awe, remembering his own first moments beneath the stone.

Two more followed that season: Mira, on May 2nd, and Solen, on May 19th.

Raising them changed Alder.

He became patient in ways he had never been before, guiding them across surfaces, showing them which plants were safe, how to retreat into their shells at the first sign of danger. Liora was steady and wise, her instincts sharp, her care constant.

But Alder did not lose his curiosity.

Instead, he began to explore in smaller arcs, always returning. He ventured to the edges of the garden, mapping its boundaries in his mind. He discovered a rusted nail near the fence, its surface rough and strangely warm in the sun. He found a patch of mint that tingled when tasted, a sensation that lingered long after he moved on.

One autumn, he encountered something extraordinary.

Near the far edge of the garden, where the soil gave way to a strip of gravel, Alder found a mirror shard.

Unlike the blue glass, this one held a perfect reflection. When he approached, he saw himself—his shell, his body, the slow, deliberate movement that defined him. He watched himself move, fascinated and unsettled.

For the first time, Alder understood that he was not just part of the world—he was something within it, distinct and visible.

He returned to the mirror often, bringing Liora and, eventually, their children. Each reacted differently. Brin was confused, Mira delighted, Solen wary.

Life continued.

The children grew, their shells strengthening, their movements becoming more confident. They began to explore on their own, venturing short distances before returning with stories of their own discoveries.

Brin found a hollow acorn cap that collected rainwater like a tiny pond. Mira discovered a patch of wildflowers beyond the fence, their colors vivid and varied. Solen, cautious but observant, mapped safe paths through the garden, noting where shadows lingered longest.

Alder watched them with pride.

Years passed, marked not by calendars but by seasons—the cycle of growth and decay, rain and dryness, warmth and cold. Alder’s shell grew heavier, its spiral more pronounced. His movements slowed, though his mind remained as curious as ever.

He and Liora often spoke of the world beyond the garden.

“Do you still want to go?” she asked one evening, as they rested beneath their cabbage leaf.

“Yes,” Alder said. “But I think… I’ve already found most of what I was looking for.”

Liora understood.

In his fifth year, Alder made one final journey.

He told Liora and the others he would return, and he believed it. But there was a quiet sense, deep within him, that this journey was different.

He moved beyond the garden, crossing the gravel strip with care. The ground was harsher there, less forgiving. He traveled slowly, resting often, but he did not stop.

He reached the path again—the one he had crossed long ago.

This time, he understood its nature. The vibrations were stronger, the sounds sharper. It was a place of danger, but also of possibility.

Alder waited until it was still.

Then he crossed.

Beyond the path, the world opened in a way he had never imagined. There were patches of wild growth, clusters of rocks, and, in the distance, something vast and shimmering.

It took him days to reach it.

It was water—a pond, still and reflective, stretching far beyond his sight. The surface mirrored the sky, just as the shard had done, but on a scale that made him feel both insignificant and profoundly connected.

Alder approached the edge.

He touched the water, feeling its coolness ripple outward. For a long time, he remained there, watching the reflections shift with the breeze.

He thought of the stone where he began, the blue glass, the garden, Liora, and their children.

He thought of how large the world had seemed, and how it still was.

Alder died on September 12th of his sixth year, at the edge of that pond.

He was alone, but not lonely.

The ground was soft beneath him, the air gentle. As his movements slowed and finally ceased, the world continued—water rippling, wind stirring, distant life moving in ways both small and vast.

Back in the garden, Liora and their children went on.

They explored, discovered, and remembered.

And sometimes, when the light caught just right on a fragment of glass or the surface of still water, it felt as though Alder was still there—moving slowly, endlessly curious, tracing the edges of a world that could never truly be finished.
